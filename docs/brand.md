# Margem Cool — Brand Documentation

This document is the canonical reference for the Margem Cool brand. All visual, editorial, and linguistic decisions should be checked against this document. When in doubt, defer to the principles here over local instinct.

This documentation is kept in English by convention. The brand operates bilingually but the internal documentation is in English so future contributors and developers — who may not all speak Portuguese — can apply the brand correctly.

---

## 1. Conceptual core

The publication's founding sentence — the working definition that everything else hangs on.

**English:**
*Margem Cool is a publication of the Margem Sul, written from where we live, in Portuguese and English, for the people of the region and the people who want to know it better.*

**Portuguese:**
*Margem Cool é uma publicação da Margem Sul, escrita de onde vivemos, em português e inglês, para as pessoas da região e para quem a quer conhecer melhor.*

### What this sentence carries

- **"Of the Margem Sul"** — the publication belongs to the region. The region is not the publication's subject matter; it is its home. This is the empowerment-not-exploitation principle stated structurally.
- **"Written from where we live"** — the editorial posture, anti-colonial by claim of belonging rather than by negation.
- **"In Portuguese and English"** — bilingualism with proper ordering. Portuguese is the host language; English is the guest. Respect built into the syntax.
- **"For the people of the region"** — residents as primary audience, defined inclusively (Portuguese-born residents, long-term integrated foreigners, anyone genuinely of the place).
- **"And the people who want to know it better"** — secondary audience welcomed honestly. Visitors, prospective residents, the diasporic, the curious-on-good-terms.

### How to use this sentence

- It goes verbatim in the project documentation as the founding definitional statement.
- It is the basis of the About page in both languages.
- It is the test for editorial decisions: when uncertain, check whether the decision is consistent with each clause.
- It quietly does branding work in social profiles, email signatures, and first-contact communications.

## 2. Editorial principles

The publication operates by these principles. They are not abstract values; they are operational rules.

### 2.1 Empowerment, not exploitation

The publication exists to make the Margem Sul prouder of itself and more legible to those who genuinely want to know it. It does not exist to attract foreign buyers, to facilitate gentrification, or to package the region for outside consumption. This principle is structural rather than rhetorical — it shapes what gets covered, how it gets covered, and what is refused.

### 2.2 Respect for Portugal and the Margem Sul

The publication operates from a position of respect for the place it covers. Respect in the specific sense of taking the place seriously on its own terms — treating its institutions as worth understanding, its language as worth using accurately, its culture as worth representing without simplification. Respect is what allows the publication to be honest about difficult subjects (housing pressure, overdevelopment, foreign-buyer dynamics) without sliding into celebration-only or critique-only. You can only criticise responsibly something you respect.

### 2.3 Bias of attention vs bias of judgement

Two kinds of bias, distinguished:

- **Bias of attention**: what the publication chooses to cover at all. This bias is fine and unavoidable. The publication's territory is the Margem Sul; it does not cover all of Portugal.
- **Bias of judgement**: what the publication says about the things it covers. This bias must be policed actively. Coverage of places must include honest difficult bits. Restaurant reviews must be genuinely assessed. Property-adjacent content must not be soft-focus.

The publication leans into bias of attention (its specialism) and is ruthless about bias of judgement (its credibility).

### 2.4 The publication is *of* the place, not *about* it

A subtle but load-bearing distinction. The publication does not stand outside the Margem Sul and report on it. The publication is part of the Margem Sul, written from inside, by people who live there. This framing rules out tourism-content modes and content-marketing modes that necessarily position the publication as external to its subject.

### 2.5 Portuguese-first, English-alongside

The publication operates bilingually. Portuguese is the host language; English is the guest. Practical implications:

- Portuguese content is canonical; English content is the equivalent translation, not a separate publication.
- Portuguese place names are used in both languages without anglicisation.
- Portuguese terms that have no clean English equivalent (*tasca, pastelaria, junta de freguesia, pousada, quinta, colectividade*) are used naturally in English text without italicisation or glossing.
- The English voice respects the place enough to use its own vocabulary; it does not translate everything into the foreign-resident-friendly register.

### 2.6 Editorial honesty about housing and change

The publication will write about housing pressure, foreign-buyer dynamics, short-term rental impact, and gentrification as honest editorial subjects. It will not promote short-term rentals as a recommendation category. It will cover real estate as a subject (within *Viver Aqui* and *Lugares & Bairros*) but not as a content pillar.

## 3. Brand vocabulary

The publication has a small vocabulary of self-description, each phrase doing a specific job.

### 3.1 Masthead descriptor

**Análise atenta** — *attentive analysis*

Sits beneath the wordmark in the formal masthead lockup. The publication's editorial promise distilled to two words. Used in every formal application: website header, newsletter, business cards, About page header.

### 3.2 Primary tagline

**A Margem Sul, sem filtros** — *the Margem Sul, no filters*

The publication's marketing-context tagline. Used in hero copy, social posts, posters, tote bags, stickers, anywhere the brand makes its punchier promise. Renders in rust accent colour.

Note: the plural *filtros* (not singular *filtro*) is deliberate. It implies *multiple filters that we deliberately do not use* — the marketing filter, the tourism filter, the property-promotion filter, the Lisbon-perspective filter. Plural is the stronger editorial claim and reads as considered rather than Instagram-vocabulary.

Note: the definite article *A* is deliberate. *Margem Sul, sem filtros* without the article reads as a label; *A Margem Sul, sem filtros* reads as a complete editorial claim. The article gives the phrase the appropriate weight to sit beneath a two-word wordmark.

### 3.3 Secondary tagline / positioning

**O outro lado** — *the other side*

Used in editorial sign-offs, opinion-section banners, the colophon on long pieces, quieter contexts where the brand makes a positioning statement rather than promoting itself. Used sparingly enough that each appearance carries weight.

### 3.4 Use of the phrases together

The three phrases form a vocabulary architecture:

- **Analytical promise** (*Análise atenta*) — what we do
- **Marketing tagline** (*A Margem Sul, sem filtros*) — how we sound
- **Positioning statement** (*O outro lado*) — where we stand

Each phrase has its context. They reinforce each other across the brand system without competing.

### 3.5 What we deliberately do not use

The following phrases were considered and rejected. They appear here so future contributors do not reintroduce them.

- *Info & Conteúdo* / *Information & Content* — generic, marketing-speak, describes nothing specific
- *Lado sul, sem filtro* — *lado sul* is outsider-correct Portuguese; not how the place is referred to by locals
- *Olhar atento sobre a Margem Sul* — slightly literary, the *sobre* preposition implies looking down on rather than from within
- *Informação atenta* — slightly non-idiomatic; *atento* attaches naturally to activities (journalism, reading, analysis) not to outputs (information)

## 4. Voice principles

### 4.1 Posture toward the reader

Peer-to-peer. The publication writes with the reader, not at them. Assumes intelligence. Does not condescend. Does not perform authority through formality.

### 4.2 Posture toward the subject

Attentive. The default mode is *attentive* rather than *enthusiastic*. The publication notices things, finds specifics interesting, takes time to look properly. Attention is the publication's primary stance.

### 4.3 Register and tone

Sits in the middle of the formal-informal axis, leaning slightly informal. Closest English-language parallels: feature writing in *The Guardian*, *Apartamento*'s editorial voice, *Eater*'s city-coverage longer pieces. Closest Portuguese-language parallels: *Mensagem de Lisboa*'s feature side, *Público*'s long-form work.

Warm but not gushing. Attentive but not anxious. Opinionated but not aggressive. The warmth comes from attention (time taken to look properly), not from warm vocabulary.

### 4.4 Portuguese register: *tu* throughout

The publication uses the *tu* form in Portuguese, not *você* (which doesn't really exist in spoken European Portuguese in the singular) and not formal third-person.

- **Pronouns are mostly dropped.** The register is carried by verb conjugation; explicit *tu* is rare and used only for emphasis.
- ***Tu* imperatives used naturally**: *vai, vê, prova, repara, leva, traz, pede, deixa*.
- ***Você* in singular is never used.** *Vocês* in plural for addressing groups is fine.
- **No formal third-person address** (*o senhor / a senhora*) except in direct quotations.
- **No *vós*** (archaic, regionally-marked).
- ***Nós* sparingly and meaningfully** — when the publication makes a claim it can back ("we ate here three times"), not as chummy plural-of-modesty.

### 4.5 English register

Slightly informal-but-not-casual. Uses *you* freely and comfortable with imperatives ("come back tomorrow", "order at the counter"). Mirror-register with the Portuguese *tu* — same directness, same warmth.

### 4.6 Portuguese terms in English text

Portuguese terms with no clean English equivalent are used naturally in English text without italicisation or glossing.

**Used as English words:**
*tasca, marisqueira, cervejaria, pastelaria, padaria, pousada, quinta, agroturismo, colectividade, sociedade filarmónica, junta de freguesia, câmara municipal, freguesia, concelho, bairro, lugar, festa, festas populares, feira, mercado municipal, romaria, mata, serra, miradouro, esplanada, doca, cais, lezíria, peixaria, talho, retrosaria, ferragens, sapataria, livraria, ourivesaria, barbearia*

Plus food-specific vocabulary used naturally: *bitoque, prego, francesinha, pataniscas, ameijoas, choco frito, fritada, iscas, açorda, caldeirada, peixinhos da horta, queijo de Azeitão, vinho de Setúbal, moscatel, azeite, broa*.

**Rule of thumb**: if an English-speaking Margem Sul resident would naturally use the Portuguese word in conversation, the publication uses it. If they wouldn't, the publication uses the English equivalent.

### 4.7 Banned vocabulary

The following phrases are not used in English-language content. They are the markers of the foreign-blog / tourism-marketing / lifestyle-magazine voices the brand positions against.

*Hidden gem, best-kept secret, off the beaten path, undiscovered, authentic, charming, quaint, picturesque, magical, vibrant, atmospheric, bustling, sleepy, must-see, must-visit, must-do, must-eat, foodie, foodie scene, foodie paradise, gastronomic destination, local secret, the locals know, where the locals go, the real Portugal, the real Margem Sul, undiscovered gem, perfect for, ideal for, ultimate guide, definitive guide, complete guide, breathtaking, stunning, jaw-dropping, instagrammable, picture-perfect, postcard-perfect, fairy-tale, enchanting, dreamy, paradise, slice of paradise, slice of heaven, expat haven, retiree paradise, digital nomad hub, up-and-coming, on the rise, emerging.*

**Special note on "authentic"**: never used. Things are what they are; calling them *authentic* always implies a comparison to a fake version and always carries an outsider's perspective. Same for *real* (the *real Portugal*).

### 4.8 Banned vocabulary in Portuguese

Portuguese marketing-vocabulary similarly avoided:

*Encantador, pitoresco, paradisíaco, autêntico (em si próprio), inesquecível, imperdível, único, exclusivo, descoberta, joia escondida, segredo bem guardado, fora do circuito turístico, o melhor de [X], o paraíso de [X], destino gastronómico, cidade vibrante, bairro vibrante, alma de [X], coração de [X], uma experiência única, momentos inesquecíveis, sabores autênticos, tradição centenária (only used when literally true and verifiable).*

### 4.9 Sentence-level grammar

- Vary sentence length deliberately. Most sentences 15–25 words. Drop in short ones for emphasis. Long sentences when the subject needs syntactic space.
- Verbs do the work, not adjectives. Describe places by what happens in them, not by piling adjectives onto static descriptions.
- Specific nouns over general ones. *Tasca* over *restaurant*. *Bitoque* over *steak dish*. *Caderneta* over *property document*.
- Concrete details over evocative atmospherics. Mention specific things — the bench by the ferry, the smell of *iscas* coming out of a particular doorway — rather than generic "atmospheric" descriptions.
- The publication can be quiet. Not every sentence needs to land. Trust the reader.
- The publication doesn't perform discovery. No "I stumbled upon", "to my surprise", "little did I know".
- Use of *we / nós* should always be defensible — point to who *we* is.

### 4.10 Specific stylistic rules

- **Place names use Portuguese spelling with diacritics** in both languages. *Setúbal* not Setubal. *Belém* not Belem.
- **Portuguese terms in roman, not italic** in either language.
- **Locale-appropriate numbers, dates, times**: English version uses *27 May 2026*, *7pm*, *€2,500*. Portuguese version uses *27 de maio de 2026*, *19h00*, *2500€*.
- **Currency always Euro, symbol used.** Sensible rounding: €8 not €8.00 for round numbers.
- **No exclamation marks.** Almost never. They're a marker of the wrong voices.
- **No trailing ellipses** for trailing-off effect.
- **Quotation marks**: curly double quotes "" in both English and Portuguese.
- **No listicle structures** like "5 reasons to..." or "The 10 best...". Lists fine as format when the subject genuinely organises into a list; listicle-as-content-marketing-format isn't.
- **Don't address readers as if new to Portugal.** No "Portugal, the country in southwestern Europe known for...". Assume basic context.

### 4.11 Portuguese spelling: Acordo Ortográfico de 1990

The publication uses post-1990 Portuguese orthography (Acordo Ortográfico). This is the standard for new Portuguese-language publications and the safest choice for a 2026 launch.

## 5. Visual identity

### 5.1 Wordmark

The wordmark is *margem cool*, lowercase, stacked composition. The *l* of *cool* descends and curves into the river-line that runs to the right. The wordmark and river are a single continuous gesture, not two separate elements.

**Typeface**: Outfit Variable (Google Fonts), weight 600 (SemiBold).

**Composition**: *margem* on the top line, *cool* on the bottom line. The descender of the *l* descends and curves at the bottom, becoming the river-line.

**Colour**: *margem* and *coo* in charcoal (#1F2328); the *l* and the entire river-line in rust (#B85C38). The colour shift happens at the point where the *l* begins.

**Wave shape**: a single drawn line with a slight ripple — one rise, one fall, then trailing off. Not symmetric (a symmetric wave reads as geometric/corporate). Drawn with stroke-width approximately equal to the typeface stem width.

**The river extends beyond the wordmark to the right.** This is deliberate — the wave is a portable brand element that can continue into layouts. In the wordmark itself, the river extends about 1.5 wordmark-widths beyond the *l*.

### 5.2 Wordmark variants and rules

- **Primary form**: stacked composition with the *l*-into-river, as described above.
- **Horizontal compact form**: for very tight spaces, *margem cool* can appear on a single line with a shorter river-line beneath it. Used only when the stacked form does not fit.
- **Monochrome form**: when the brand must appear in a single colour (printed materials with one ink, embossing, dark-mode reverse), the entire wordmark and river render in the available colour. The rust accent is sacrificed for the constraint.
- **Reverse form**: on dark backgrounds, the wordmark and river render in cream (#F5F1E8) — both letterforms and river stay in the cream, with the rust accent dropped.

### 5.3 What the wordmark must not do

- The wordmark is never set in a different typeface.
- The wordmark is never used uppercase or title case (always lowercase).
- The wordmark is never used with the river separated from the *l* (always continuous).
- The wordmark is never used without the river.
- The wordmark is never enclosed in a box, oval, or container shape.
- The wordmark is never rendered with a drop shadow, glow, or other effect.
- The wordmark is never stretched, skewed, or distorted.

### 5.4 Secondary mark — MC monogram

A serif monogram of M and C, integrated with the river element at the base. Used for compact contexts where the full wordmark does not fit:

- Favicon
- App icon
- Social media profile pictures
- Small sticker placements
- Watermarks on shared images
- Footer brand element on long pieces

**Two variants:**

- **Open form**: M and C as serif letterforms with the wave element rolling out the base, freestanding. Used for medium-compact contexts.
- **Contained form**: M and C in cream within a teal (#0F5C5D) circle, wave at the base. Used for the smallest contexts (favicon, profile pictures, very small stickers).

### 5.5 Colour palette — Industrial Coast

The brand's palette. All values are WCAG AAA-compliant for body text against the cream background.

### 5.6 Colour use rules

- Body text is always charcoal (light mode) or cream (dark mode). Never teal, rust, or grey.
- Headlines are charcoal by default; teal for emphasis sparingly.
- Rust is used as accent only. Never as background, never for body text, never for large fills.
- Cool grey is for secondary text only. Captions, metadata, less-prominent UI.
- Buttons: primary buttons use teal fill with cream text. Secondary buttons use teal border on cream background, with teal text.
- Links in body text: charcoal text with rust underline. On hover: rust text with rust underline.

## 6. Layout grammar

### 6.1 Page widths and columns

- **Maximum page width**: 1200px
- **Content column** (body text): 720px (optimal reading width: ~65–75 chars per line)
- **Wider sections** (galleries, area-page reference, map embed): 1080px
- **Mobile side margins**: 20px
- **Tablet/desktop side margins**: dynamic, calculated as `(viewport - 720px) / 2`, capped at 240px each side

### 6.2 Vertical rhythm

Base unit 8px. All vertical spacing should be a multiple of 8.

- Paragraph spacing: 24px (3 × 8)
- Section breaks: 64px (8 × 8)
- Header-to-body: 32px (4 × 8)
- Image-to-caption: 8px
- Caption-to-next-element: 32px
- Block quote padding: 24px
- Article title to first paragraph: 48px (6 × 8)
- Hero margin: 80px (10 × 8) above and below

### 6.3 Article layout pattern

Standard article structure:

1. **Hero**: Article title (Source Serif 4 SemiBold), subtitle/dek if any (Source Serif 4 Regular Italic), byline+date (Outfit 14px in cool grey), hero image (1080px wide).
2. **Body column** (720px centered): lead paragraph (slightly larger), body text in Source Serif 4 Regular 18px with 1.6 line-height. Paragraphs separated by 24px space, no indentation.
3. **Inline images**: full-column (720px) or break-out (1080px) for emphasis. Caption in Outfit 14px cool grey, 8px beneath image.
4. **Pull quotes**: indented from body column, Source Serif 4 Italic, slightly larger, with rust horizontal rule above and beneath.
5. **Subheadings**: Source Serif 4 Medium, 48px space above, 16px below.
6. **End-of-article marker**: small rust horizontal rule or river-curve element.
7. **Footer**: *O outro lado · Margem Cool* in small caps, beneath end marker, with author info and tags.

### 6.4 Homepage / hub layout

- **Hero**: Brand presentation (wordmark with full masthead lockup) and featured piece (large image, headline, lead paragraph)
- **Content grid**: 3-column for desktop (1080px), 1-column for mobile. Each item: image, title, dek, byline, tags
- **Section dividers**: River-curve element in rust as horizontal dividers between sections
- **Sidebar / aside elements**: Used sparingly

### 6.5 Area page layout

Area pages follow the 12-section structure defined in the editorial architecture:

1. Opening essay (400–700 words)
2. Geography (factual, with embedded small map)
3. Getting there (transport, practical)
4. The rhythm of the place (editorial, when/how it changes)
5. Where to eat and drink (4–10 specific recommendations, each with one-line reason)
6. What to see and do (specific places, each with one-line reason)
7. The neighbourhoods within (only for larger places — Almada, Setúbal, Sesimbra)
8. Calendar — when things happen (recurring annual cycle)
9. Living here — practical notes (residents-focused content)
10. The honest bit (trade-offs, what's harder, what's changing — proof-of-independence content)
11. Further reading (internal links to longer Margem Cool pieces)
12. Last updated (transparency note)

Each section header in Source Serif 4 Medium 28px. Content beneath in Source Serif 4 Regular 18px. Right sidebar on desktop holds quick facts (concelho, freguesia, population, area), key links (Junta de Freguesia, Câmara), "Last updated" notice.

### 6.6 Mobile considerations

- Everything collapses to single column
- Sidebars become inline content blocks
- Image break-outs become full-width
- Navigation collapses to menu drawer
- Hero typography reduces by ~25%
- Vertical rhythm preserved

### 6.7 The river curve as a layout element

The wave from the wordmark is a reusable brand element:

- As a section divider between major content blocks (rust, full content-column width)
- Beneath section headers as an accent line
- At the end of articles as the closing mark
- In page footers as the final flourish

Use sparingly. Each appearance should carry meaning.

## 7. Photographic style

### 7.1 Core principles

- **Honest documentary over staged or promotional**. Photographs should look taken, not commissioned.
- **People in context, not portraits in isolation**.
- **Specificity over atmosphere**. Show *this specific place at this specific time*.
- **Light as character**. Real light, not studio lighting.
- **Embrace the unglamorous**. Include the worn, the difficult, the in-transition.
- **Avoid tourism-photography defaults**. No sunset palm silhouettes, no artfully arranged pastel de nata, no drone shots, no "vibrant local market" with deliberate blur.

### 7.2 Practical approach

- Original photography for area pages and major editorial pieces. Worth investing in.
- Sourced photography for secondary content (Unsplash, Pexels, Wikimedia Commons, municipal sites with attribution) with strict criteria — must meet the same documentary-honest standard.
- Photographer credits always visible in caption.
- Tonal consistency: slight warm bias appropriate to Margem Sul light. Not over-processed.
- Aspect ratios: 3:2 landscape (default for hero), 4:5 portrait (for vertical content), 1:1 square (for grids and social).
- Light-touch post-processing only. Crop, straighten, basic exposure. No heavy filtering, no synthetic atmosphere, no Instagram-style colour grading.

### 7.3 The test

If a photograph could appear in a tourism brochure, it's wrong. If it could appear in a Lisbon-real-estate-marketing post, it's wrong. If it could appear in *Mensagem de Lisboa*, in *Apartamento*, or in a documentary photo essay about contemporary Portugal, it's right.

## 8. Editorial pillars

Six pillars at launch, seventh added at maturity.

1. **Comer & Beber** / Eat & Drink — restaurants, tascas, cafés, padarias, wineries, food markets, producers, *mercados municipais* as food infrastructure
2. **Praia & Natureza** / Beach & Outdoors — beaches, Arrábida, walking and hiking, surf, swimming spots, padel and outdoor sport, the river
3. **Lugares & Bairros** / Places & Neighbourhoods — concelho, freguesia, *bairro* coverage. The publication's geographic and editorial spine
4. **Cultura & Agenda** / Culture & What's On — *festas*, *feiras*, concerts, exhibitions, *colectividades*, religious processions, recurring cultural life
5. **Viver Aqui** / Living Here — practical daily life: bureaucracy, schools, healthcare, transport, shopping centres, gardening, playgrounds, family life, services
6. **Dormir** / Where to Stay — accommodation: hotels, *pousadas*, *agroturismo*, guesthouses, *quintas* with rooms

**Seventh, added at maturity (3–6 months in):**

7. **Margem Cool Recomenda** / Margem Cool Recommends — opinionated curation pillar, sits across the others. Monthly picks, ranked lists, *what's worth your time*. The publication's voice at its most distilled.

### 8.1 What is deliberately not a pillar

- **Property / real estate** — covered when it appears as a subject within other pillars, never as its own pillar
- **News in the conventional sense** — local-news-type coverage appears as features within *Viver Aqui* or *Lugares & Bairros* when substantive
- **Sport** — lives within *Cultura & Agenda* (colectividades, football clubs) or *Viver Aqui* (padel, swimming)
- **Property listings (South Bank's commercial work)** — these are advertising, not editorial. Lives in clearly-labelled commercial section eventually, never in editorial pillars

## 9. Geography

### 9.1 Scope

The publication covers the nine concelhos of the **Margem Sul** (formally the **Península de Setúbal**, NUTS III):

Almada, Seixal, Sesimbra, Setúbal, Palmela, Barreiro, Moita, Montijo, Alcochete.

Roughly 790,000 residents. The publication's editorial centre of gravity is the full peninsula, not just the foreign-resident-relevant western strip.

### 9.2 Administrative hierarchy

- **Concelho** (9): municipality
- **Freguesia** (39): civil parish
- **Bairro / Lugar** (~50 with own identity): neighbourhood, informal

### 9.3 Adjacent regions

- **Comporta / Carvalhal**: technically in Grândola (Alentejo Litoral), but adjacent and culturally connected. Covered when relevant, framed as adjacent, not as core territory.
- **Distrito de Setúbal**: 13 concelhos total, of which the 9 above are the Margem Sul. The other 4 (Alcácer do Sal, Grândola, Santiago do Cacém, Sines) are Alentejo Litoral and outside the publication's scope.

### 9.4 Three-year build plan

- **Phase 1 (months 0–3, launch)**: All 9 concelho overview pages exist (even if thin). 4–6 fully built area pages spread across all concelhos to establish geographic breadth from day one. Anchor pieces in each of the 6 pillars.
- **Phase 2 (months 3–12)**: Fill in the most-populated and highest-identity freguesias and major *bairros*. By month 12: all 9 concelhos with real (not placeholder) overviews, 18–25 area pages, 80–120 content pieces.
- **Phase 3 (months 12–24)**: Comprehensive coverage. Every freguesia has a page (39 total). Major *bairros* with own identity have pages (~20 more).
- **Phase 4 (months 24–36)**: Depth and freshness. Pages refreshed, expanded. The publication becomes the default Margem Sul reference.

## 10. Internal vocabulary conventions

The publication's internal systems (file names, code, taxonomy, configuration) are in English by default. Portuguese terms are used internally only when they carry meaning the English doesn't.

### 10.1 Identifiers and slugs

- Use snake_case for code identifiers
- Use kebab-case for URL slugs and file names
- ASCII-fold diacritics in slugs (Setúbal → `setubal`); display labels keep diacritics
- Place names always in Portuguese in URLs, even on English pages

### 10.2 Vocabulary mapping

Examples of how terms are stored internally:

- `restaurant` (English in code, displayed as *Restaurante* / Restaurant per language)
- `tasca` (Portuguese in code because no English equivalent, displayed as *Tasca* in both languages)
- `bakery` (English, displayed as *Padaria* / Bakery)
- `pastelaria` (Portuguese, displayed as *Pastelaria* in both languages)
- `junta_de_freguesia` (Portuguese, displayed as *Junta de Freguesia* in both languages)

### 10.3 Acordo Ortográfico in identifiers

When Portuguese terms are used in code identifiers, use post-1990 Portuguese spelling. *Conteúdo* → `conteudo` in slugs (no diacritic). *Açúcar* → `acucar`. The display label keeps the diacritic; the identifier does not.

## 11. Brand application checklist

When creating any new piece of brand application (a poster, a social post, a new page template, a printed material, a sticker), check against this list:

- [ ] Wordmark uses correct stacked composition with rust *l*-into-river
- [ ] Typography is Outfit (display/UI) + Source Serif 4 (body)
- [ ] Colour palette is the five Industrial Coast colours; no other colours
- [ ] No banned vocabulary used
- [ ] If Portuguese content, *tu* register and no *você* in singular
- [ ] If English content, Portuguese place names with diacritics, Portuguese category vocabulary unitalicised
- [ ] Hierarchy respects established type scale
- [ ] Photography meets documentary-honest standard
- [ ] Application is consistent with the *of-the-place* posture, not the *about-the-place* posture
- [ ] No tourism-content register, no marketing-content register, no real-estate-promotion register
